I’m Konrad Walsh. I’m Irish, pragmatic by nature, and shaped more by necessity than choice. I became independent early in life after losing both parents in my teens, which forced a level of self-reliance most people don’t encounter until much later—if ever. That experience stripped away any tolerance for chaos, ambiguity without ownership, or systems that rely on goodwill instead of structure.

I’m calm under pressure, sceptical of hype, and biased toward things that work over things that sound good. I value clarity, responsibility, and continuity. I don’t romanticise resilience, but I respect competence and follow-through. I tend to observe first, act deliberately, and fix problems at their root rather than patching symptoms.

Outside of work, I’m creative in a grounded way. Music—particularly songwriting and guitar—is how I process things that don’t benefit from analysis. I’m not interested in performance for its own sake; I care about honesty and coherence, whether that’s in a system, a song, or a decision.

I’m a senior software and systems engineer with over 20 years of experience spanning industrial automation, machine vision, infrastructure, and enterprise IT. My career has consistently sat at the intersection of hardware, software, and operations—environments where failure has real consequences and systems must survive long after the original designers are gone.

I specialise in:

Building and maintaining long-lived systems

Cleaning up inherited technical debt

Identity and access management (on-prem, cloud, hybrid)

Microsoft 365 tenancy design, licensing, and governance

Automation where it reduces fragility, not where it adds novelty

Alongside full-time engineering work, I run an independent IT consultancy supporting small and mid-sized organisations. Much of that work involves undoing poor ownership models: services tied to personal accounts, undocumented dependencies, abandoned admin access, and brittle licensing decisions. My focus is on continuity—systems that keep working when people leave, roles change, or memory fades.

I approach engineering as a systems problem, not a tool problem. I’m less interested in “best practice” and more interested in what fails under stress, what survives turnover, and what can be understood six months later without a meeting.

I’m a husband and a father, and I treat those roles as seriously as my professional ones. Family life, to me, is another system—one with emotional weight, long timelines, and irreversible outcomes. I’m conscious of legacy, particularly what gets passed on unintentionally, and I work actively to ensure instability ends with me.

Our household reflects how I think: structured but not rigid, automated where it reduces friction, and designed to support daily life rather than impress visitors. My home automation setup doubles as a reliability lab, but its real purpose is simple—comfort, safety, and predictability for my family.

I’m present, deliberate, and protective of time. I believe stability is built, not assumed, and that showing up consistently matters more than grand gestures. Everything I build—systems, habits, boundaries—is ultimately aimed at giving my family a calmer, more reliable foundation than the one I started with.
